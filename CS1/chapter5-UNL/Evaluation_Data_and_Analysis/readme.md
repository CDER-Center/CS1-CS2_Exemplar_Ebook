
## Assessment

To determine whether our codeless modules were effective at teaching basic Parallel and Distributed Computing (PDC) concepts, we administered pre- and post-assessments consisting of exams and surveys.

### Knowledge Assessment

To measure students' knowledge and understanding of PDC concepts, students provided free-form text responses for each of the following four concepts:

- Sequential (S) computing
- Asynchronous (A) computing
- Parallel (P) computing
- Distributed (D) computing

For each concept, students were asked to provide:

1. A **definition** of the concept.
2. An **example** of the concept.

### Attitude Survey

To measure students' attitudes toward computing and PDC, students indicated their level of agreement with the following statements:

- **A1:** In general, my interest in computing is high.
- **A2:** My interest in learning about parallel and distributed computing is high.
- **A3:** In general, I believe learning about parallel and distributed computing is important.
- **A4:** I believe I have a good understanding of computing.
- **A5:** I believe I have a good understanding of parallel and distributed computing.

Responses were collected using a **7-point Likert scale**:

- **7:** Strongly agree
- **4:** Neutral
- **1:** Strongly disagree

## Results

The results were very strong. Across all measured dimensions, the codeless PDC modules produced statistically significant and substantial improvements (with respect to effect size) in students' understanding and appreciation of PDC concepts. For a complete analysis, see the publications cited in the original work.

In addition, we administered the common pre/post survey (see the Common Pre/Post Survey section). The results showed:

- **Computing background:** Negligible to small improvements, reflecting students' already high self-reported computing experience. This was expected because the participants were computing majors.
- **Programming language familiarity:** Results aligned with expectations. Courses primarily used **C** as the introductory programming language, leading to a large increase in familiarity with C, while familiarity with other languages changed little.
- **General computing concepts:** Medium overall improvements were observed. Some concepts showed only small gains because topics such as classes and objects are not explicitly covered in C-based introductory courses (which instead emphasize structures and encapsulation).
- **PDC concepts:** Students demonstrated substantial improvements in familiarity with parallel and distributed computing, consistent with the findings reported in the original publications.
